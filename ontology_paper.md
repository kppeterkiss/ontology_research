## The canonic way to solve the problem:
Candidate generation:
- According to Gemini To ensure your candidate generation is highly accurate, combine these two vector matches (avg or some weighting):
  - Term-to-Term Similarity: Embed the raw candidate word from the text and compare it to the existing concept names and synonyms in your ontology.
  - Context-to-Definition Similarity: Embed the whole sentence/paragraph and compare it to your concept definitions.
  - we can do thresholding on the similarity scores. 
  - According to gemini no need to fine tune the model.

NIL Prediction problem: LLM excells at it with a catch:
  - Where General LLMs Excel (The Positives)
    - Deep Semantic Alignment: An LLM easily recognizes that "Apis mellifera" and "Western Honey Bee" are the same concept, even if their spellings share 0% lexical similarity. It will correctly mark this as EXISTING.
    - Contextual Disambiguation: If the text says "I need to clean the queen's cell," the LLM knows based on the context of your beekeeping ontology that this does not refer to a cellular phone or a prison cell. It will match it to your existing Queen Cell node.
  - Where General LLMs Fail (The Bottlenecks)
    - The "Hallucination/Compliance" Bias: LLMs have a natural bias to try to please you. If you give them a list of 5 existing concepts and a completely new term, the LLM will often try to warp its own logic to force a match with one of those 5 concepts rather than choosing NEW (NIL).
    - Context Window Limits: If your ontology grows to 5,000 concepts, you cannot paste all 5,000 concepts into a single Llama prompt. The model will suffer from "lost in the middle" phenomena and miss matches.
